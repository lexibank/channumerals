Other comments: Sora use an old duodecimal numeral system before. The system is unique in building by dozens /miɟɡəl/ 12 within scores /-koɽi/ 20 as far as 399, i.e.

to a score of scores less one.  (Other Munda systems build by tens w/in scores to 399.)  The origin of /koɽi/, which is common to unrelated languages of NE India, is uncertain.  An Indo-Aryan word /sɔɑ/ ‘hundred’ is now used as an alternate base

from 100.  There is also a native base /-mɑɽiɲ/ ‘thousand; a measure of about one quart’.  The transcription is phonemic.

 

A special counting system of the South Munda language Sora [SRB]

Monosi Raika of Sogeda, Rayagada Dt., Orissa, India

Patricia J. Donegan and David Stampe of University of Hawaii at Manoa

<donegan@hawaii.com>, <stampe@hawaii.com>

This system, which is used for counting days of agricultural work given or owed, is based
on /siɁi/ (compound form -si) ‘hand’ and /ɟeɁeŋ/ (compound form -ɟeŋ ‘foot’), and is
perhaps the prototype for pental systems (as in Khmer), and vigesimal systems.  The twenty
base form is not */bɑr-ɟeŋ/ ‘two-hand’, nor is it /bɔ-koɽi/ ‘one score’, as in ordinary counting,
but /bɔ-ɖɑŋɡo/ ‘one stick’, perhaps a token, or perhaps an abbreviation of  /ɖɑŋɡo-si/
‘entire arm, lit. stick hand’.  For pragmatic reasons the system is not used for very high
numbers, but it is in principle possible to count to/mɨ-ɟeŋ unɟi ɖɑŋɡo mɨ-ɟeŋ unɟi/ ‘399’.  
The transcription is phonemic.

The Phonemes of the South Munda language Sora [SRB]

Monosi Raika of Sogeda, Rayagada Dt., Orissa, India

Patricia J. Donegan and David Stampe of University of Hawai‘i/Manoa,

Honolulu, Hawaii, USA <donegan@hawaii.com>, <stampe@hawaii.com>

Sora is an OV language with a polysynthetic VO morphology.  Phrase accent is initial,
and word accent is stem-initial, though an initial light stem syllable may be anacrustic
before a heavy (C)VC syllable.  Stems except verbs are polysyllabic, but noninitial
elements of compounds are monosyllabic, and there is a rich noun allomorphy to meet
this condition.  Words have prefixes, infixes, and many suffixes.  Syllables are (C)V(C),
where C are in the Consonant chart and V in the Vowel chart.  Of consonants, voiceless
 consonants are never final, and voiced ones are checked syllable-finally (a Munda trait),
and in Sora final /ɡ/ no longer exists; the flap is only between vowels, the glottal only
between morpheme-internal identical vowels that can occur morpheme-finally, i.e. any
but /ɨ/ and /ə/.  Of the vowels, /u/ is spread-labialized vowel as in Japanese or Swedish
(there is no IPA symbol), and initial /ə/ if accented is [ɑ].
