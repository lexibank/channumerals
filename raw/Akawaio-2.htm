Other comments: In Inkarîkok there are several ways to organizing the cardinal system. I consider two groups: Old Fashion and New Fashion. The first group includes old man and the second includes teachers, students and young people.  Old Model includes the base of a hand. This means after 4 (upto four) you have a unit (a handful =5). After 5 the count reference is always putting one hand in front of the other hand. In my dissertation I considered that the Cardinal Inkarîkok system would be based on quinary. But today I think the system is binary or at most tertiary. Since the count after 3 becomes the repetition of 2 = 4 which lexical source are number 2. The system closes when you complete a unity Set 20= one man (TIWIN KAKPON = 2 handfuls and 2 feetfuls).

Note that:

a- (two possibilities) b- translate: miyak=hand; putu=finger, pîta=foot, pona=to, KAKPON (person, people from sky).

Other comments: In Inkarîkok there are several ways to organizing the cardinal system. New fashion indicates the different possibilities to organize the numeral system described by the younger speakers who act as teachers in the schools of the region Inkarîkok. The description below shows a numerical system in full SET. After (up to four) number 4 the speakers started witch the base's unit 5 = (a handful), the finger (s) and then the base 2.

Other higher numbers:

21: tiwin kakpon pona tiwin turënne kakpon inna putu

22. tiwin kakpon pona asakrë turënne kakpon inna putu

23. tiwin kakpon pona osoruwau turënne kakpon inna putu 

24. tiwin kakpon pona asakërëʔne turënne kakpon inna putu

25. tiwin kakpon pona miyak turënne kakpon miyak

26. tiwin kakpon pona miyak turënne kakpon miyak tiwin miyak putu

27. tiwin kakpon pona miyak turënne kakpon miyak asakrë miyak putu

28. tiwin kakpon pona miyak turënne kakpon miyak osoruwau miyak putu

29. tiwin kakpon pona miyak turënne kakpon miyak asakërëʔne miyak putu

30. asakrë kakpon pona asakrë turënne kakpon inna

40.  asakrë kakpon

80.  asakrë kakpon pona  asakrë kakpon

Note that:  a- translate: inna=her or his hand (possession form).

Partial Conclusion: Although it is a relatively simple numerical system, the possibilities of combinations that result in variations make the system complex especially when the suffixes appear -ne e -nan (Plural affirmative). It is also not clear whether the plural forms occurring in the second position are optional or not, for example:

Asakërëknan pona asakërëkne asakrëkne ipona = 12

Asakërëknan pona asakërëkne asakrëknan pona osoruwau ipona = 15

The complexity of this system also forms by the name in the Possessed form miyak (inna=her-his hand), Also by postposition in possessed form (i-pona), etc.

Let's think about the two possibilities for this cardinal system:

1) First hypothesis: that both base-2 and -5 effects derive from general constraints of symmetry. I am meaning (two hands side by side and two feet side by side, one man side by side).  

2) The second hypothesis: It is a kind of the system of base 2, if I considered that number 3 has a little morphology from number 2. Although I have not yet discovered the motivation of number 3.

18. osoruwau miyaʔ pona osoruwau, lit: 'three hands missing three fingers (foot)

Other comments: In Ingarikó language, hands and feet are references for numbering, consisting of 1, 2, 3 and 4 units counting by fingers, and the number 5 is considered the whole unit - one hand, which is translated to Portuguese language as 5 fingers in a hand. After that, for instance, the number 6 follows this logic: it´s missing one finger to complete the whole two hands. And so this goes to count the fingers of the feet. As long as the hands and feet are all complete then, one whole unit is done which means a man. The Ingarikó people are trying to develop a numeric system from that point of view, therefore all other numbers after 20 is still under construction.

This logic system is like the Dutch, German and French system, that numbering is done not by adding, but absencing (lacking). 

Note that the symbol (ʔ) is a glottal phoneme.

Other comments: Akawaio has four numbers 1 to 4 still in use. Some have told us that for five, they used to say "one hand", and then six is "one hand one", seven is  "one hand two", eight is "one hand three", nine is "one hand four" and ten is "two hands". Suffice it to say that the Akawaio don't have extensive counters, and their culture shows an incredible indifference to being exact with numbers. It's just not important to them. The above additional numbers from 5 to 20 were recorded in the book ' An Introduction to the Akawaio and Arekuna People' editorː Walter F. Edwards, University of Guyana, 1977. It seemed they can to count up to eighty-five follow this pattern, but this clumsy old counting was not used any more. Note that the (') mark in numbers 1 to 4 represents stress and is on the syllable following the mark.
